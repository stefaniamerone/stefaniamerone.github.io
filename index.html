Research

Trust, but Verify: a Theory of Selective Verification (JMP)

Abstract

I develop a theory of selective source verification in which Bayesian agents optimally choose whether to verify the credibility of information. The model shows that asymmetric fact-checking can arise under fully rational behavior, without relying on cognitive biases, and characterizes how motivated reasoning and confirmatory bias alter verification incentives.

Keywords: Bayesian learning, source verification, motivated reasoning, confirmatory bias

Commitment and the Identification of Temptation

R. Suchy, S. Toussaert

Abstract

Commitment is widely used as evidence of temptation: if individuals restrict their future options, they are presumed to anticipate self-control problems. Empirically, however, commitment is typically measured as a binary decision to remove an option, collapsing rich preference information into a single statistic and risking systematic misclassification of underlying motives. We address this limitation in a field experiment on a food-ordering platform. Participants rank all subsets of three meal categories, and one chosen menu is later implemented for a binding order. Observing both singleton preferences and the full lattice of menus allows us to disentangle normative tastes, flexibility motives, and temptation channels. We show that temptation is identified only relative to normative preferences and that unidimensional self-control models impose sharp restrictions frequently violated in the data.

Keywords: Temptation, commitment, flexibility, self-control, field experiment

Continuity, Convexity, Centrality: Axiomatizing and Comparing Location Indices

Abstract

I provide an axiomatic foundation of location indices, characterizing the class of statistics that minimize a continuous and convex expected loss. The resulting class includes familiar measures such as the mean and, in the limit, the median. Interpreting the recovered loss function through a decision-theoretic lens, I show how its asymmetry captures first-order risk attitudes while its convexity determines sensitivity to extreme observations. I also discuss when these underlying features can be identified from observed behavior.

Keywords: Location indices, axiomatic foundations, risk preferences, robust statistics
